Convective Heat Transfer & Nusselt Number Analysis

Experimentally characterized forced convection from aluminum cylinders in a wind tunnel, developed a custom empirical correlation, and benchmarked it against established theoretical models.

Project Overview

Measured convective heat transfer coefficients and Nusselt numbers for three aluminum cylinder diameters across a wide range of Reynolds numbers, then fit a custom empirical correlation to the data.

"The custom curve-fit (Nu = 0.590·Re⁰·⁵⁴⁰·Pr¹/³) achieved an R² of 0.994 and an average error of 3.1% — compared to 28.5% and 32.0% for the Churchill-Bernstein and Hilpert correlations, respectively — demonstrating the value of site-specific empirical calibration."

This experiment studies forced convection from cylinders in crossflow, a configuration common in heat exchangers, electronics cooling, and structural thermal analysis. By testing three diameters (12.72 mm, 17.15 mm, and 19.12 mm) across velocities from 2 to 40 m/s, the experiment isolates the effects of both geometry and flow speed on heat transfer performance.

Experimental Setup

Each aluminum cylinder was pre-heated using hot packs to above 50°C, then quickly placed into a wind tunnel test stand. A centerline thermocouple recorded temperature as the cylinder cooled, and the elapsed time to drop from 40°C to 32°C was measured for each velocity and diameter combination. Atmospheric pressure was logged at each run to correct air density and kinematic viscosity.

Equipment

Atmospheric Pressure Correction

The lab is located at elevation, with measured pressures around 84.4–85.3 kPa — well below sea level. This directly shifts air density and kinematic viscosity, which in turn shifts the calculated Reynolds numbers. Ignoring this correction would introduce systematic error in all correlation comparisons.

Analysis Approach

All calculations were performed in Python using numpy and scipy. Air properties were interpolated at the film temperature for each data point, then corrected for local atmospheric pressure.

1

Heat Transfer Coefficient

Applied the lumped capacitance model to extract h from the measured cooling interval, including end-cap area contributions. Biot number verified to be < 0.1 for all cases, confirming the approximation is valid.

2

Reynolds & Nusselt Numbers

Re was computed using pressure-corrected kinematic viscosity at the film temperature. Nu was derived from h, cylinder diameter, and air thermal conductivity.

3

Uncertainty Propagation

Uncertainty in h was propagated analytically from measurement uncertainties in diameter (±0.1 mm), cooling time (±0.5 s), and temperature (±0.2°C). Nu uncertainty scaled accordingly, with diameter uncertainty doubled due to its squared dependence.

4

Power-Law Curve Fit

Log-log linear regression across all diameters and velocities yielded a single best-fit correlation of the form Nu = C·Rem·Pr1/3, with C and m extracted after removing Pr dependence.

5

Correlation Benchmarking

Churchill-Bernstein and Hilpert predictions were computed for each data point and compared against experimental Nu values, with percent errors reported at each condition and averaged across all runs.

Results

Figure 1 — Heat Transfer Coefficient vs. Air Velocity

Upload Figure 1 here h vs. Velocity plot (all 3 diameters, with error bars)

The heat transfer coefficient increases non-linearly with air velocity across all three diameters, consistent with forced convection theory. Smaller diameter cylinders show consistently higher h values, a consequence of thinner thermal boundary layers and larger surface-area-to-volume ratios. Error bars — primarily driven by timing uncertainty — remain proportionally constant across the velocity range, confirming that the trends are statistically meaningful and not artifacts of measurement noise.

Figure 2 — Nusselt vs. Reynolds Number with Custom Curve-Fit

Upload Figure 2 here Nu vs. Re (log scale) with power-law curve-fit line

Experimental Nusselt numbers plotted against Reynolds number collapse tightly onto a single power-law curve across all three cylinder diameters, demonstrating that geometry-normalized scaling captures the dominant physics. The best-fit correlation Nu = 0.590·Re0.540·Pr1/3 achieves an R² of 0.994, with an average error of 3.1% and a maximum of 7.8%. The tight fit across a 10× range of Reynolds numbers validates the lumped-capacitance approach and confirms that this custom correlation is well-suited to these specific test conditions.

Figure 3 — Comparison with Churchill-Bernstein & Hilpert Correlations

Upload Figure 3 here Nu vs. Re with Churchill-Bernstein and Hilpert lines

Both standard correlations systematically underpredict the experimental Nusselt numbers across the full Reynolds number range, with average errors of 28.5% (Churchill-Bernstein) and 32.0% (Hilpert). The discrepancy is most pronounced at lower velocities and remains proportional as Reynolds number increases, suggesting a consistent offset rather than a modeling breakdown at specific flow regimes. The likely cause is elevated turbulence intensity in the wind tunnel and finite end-effects on the cylinders — neither of which are captured in the idealized "infinite cylinder in uniform crossflow" assumptions underlying both correlations. This reinforces the importance of empirical calibration when precision is required.

Key Achievements

High-Accuracy Empirical Correlation

Derived a power-law Nu–Re correlation with R² = 0.994 and 3.1% average error, substantially outperforming standard textbook models for this test setup.

Full Uncertainty Analysis

Analytically propagated measurement uncertainties through every calculated quantity, providing statistically meaningful error bars on all 42 data points.

End-to-End Python Analysis

Built a complete data pipeline in Python — from raw thermocouple readings through air property interpolation, pressure correction, regression, and publication-quality plots.

Correlation Gap Identified

Quantified a consistent 28–32% underprediction by Churchill-Bernstein and Hilpert models, attributable to wind tunnel turbulence and finite end effects not captured in idealized formulations.
